01 — Executive Summary

Lake Erie Golf Cars

The Demand Is Real

Over 2,100 people per year reach out through the website to buy golf carts, request parts, or schedule service. The demand is there — sales, parts, and service all coming through the site.

What's Getting in the Way

The measurement system is reporting nearly double the conversions that are happening, so Google's algorithms are optimizing against the wrong actions. The highest-traffic organic content attracts non-buyers. Buyer-intent keywords sit on page 2–6 of Google. Paid social produces roughly 26 conversions from 5,000 sessions.

The Blind Spots

56% of Google Ads conversions aren't form submissions. Phone calls — likely a significant channel for a dealership with two showrooms — aren't tracked at all. 58% of traffic arrives on mobile, which converts at less than half the desktop rate.

The Foundation Is Strong — The Infrastructure Needs to Match. Forty years of reputation, 100+ golf course relationships, an authorized E-Z-GO dealership, and 176 real lead events per month. What's missing is the website architecture to build authority, measure accurately, and convert the demand that's already been earned. The current platform creates a ceiling — the fix is a new build designed around how buyers search and shop for golf carts.

Mobile drives 58% of traffic but only 37% of conversions. Desktop converts at 2.5x the rate of mobile. Looking at the current mobile site, this gap isn't surprising — the site is not optimized for mobile. The layout, navigation, and form experience on a phone make it harder for visitors to take action.

What's Likely Happening

Mobile visitors tend to be in research mode — browsing inventory, checking prices, comparing options. When they're ready to act, many will either call (which we can't track yet) or come back on a desktop to fill out a form. The mobile experience compounds this — multi-field forms are difficult to complete on a phone, there's no prominent click-to-call button, and mobile users who want to reach out have to navigate to a form page instead of just tapping to call.

The Opportunity

This gap doesn't mean mobile visitors are lower quality — it means the mobile experience can be optimized to meet them where they are. Faster load times, simplified mobile forms, and a prominent click-to-call button are the typical fixes. Once phone call tracking is in place, we'd likely see that many of these "non-converting" mobile visitors are converting — just by phone instead of form.

There's a significant gap between what Google Ads reports as conversions (858) and what we can verify as actual form submissions through GA4 (377). That's a 56% difference — 481 of those reported conversions are not real form submissions.

Why this matters in plain terms: Google Ads uses an automated bidding system called "Smart Bidding" — it automatically decides who sees ads and how much to pay per click based on who it thinks will convert. When the system believes 858 people converted, it learns from all 858 to find more people like them. But if only 377 of those were real form submissions, the system is learning from 481 signals that aren't real leads. It's a bit like a GPS that counts every stop sign as "you've arrived" — it starts routing you to intersections instead of destinations.

10 — Campaign Performance

Campaign Breakdown & Structure

Campaign Performance

There are two types of Google Ads campaigns running here:

  • Search campaigns: You choose specific keywords (like "new golf carts for sale"). When someone searches those words, your ad appears at the top of Google's search results. You have direct control over which searches trigger your ads.
  • Performance Max (PMax): You provide Google with a budget, images, and headlines. Google's AI then decides where to show your ads — search results, YouTube, Gmail, Google Maps, and more. The upside is broader reach. The tradeoff is less control.

What We See in the Campaign Structure

  1. "New Golf Carts Search" and "New Golf Carts Search Camp" are two campaigns with combined spend of $3,927 that target overlapping keywords. When two campaigns in the same account go after the same searches, Google enters both into the same auction. Only one can show, but the learning data and budget get split between them instead of being consolidated. Merging these into one campaign gives the algorithm more data to work with.
  2. The PMax campaign reports 352.8 conversions on $4,157 spend. Based on what we found about the conversion setup — where only 3 of 11 counted actions are actual form submissions — a significant portion of that 352.8 number includes auto-tracked actions like map clicks and store visit estimates. PMax served ads for queries like "golf courses near me," "putt putt in cleveland," and "ohio state parks with rv camping" — searches from people who aren't shopping for golf carts.
  3. Express 6 and Express 4 are separate campaigns with higher CPCs ($3.21–$3.29) and lower volume. Consolidating these into one campaign with model-specific ad groups would give Google more data to optimize with.
  4. 14 paused campaigns from 2021–2022 (Boat Show, RV Fest, HBA Home Garden Show) are still in the account. Not actively spending, but worth cleaning up for clarity.
  5. Some ad spend goes to competitor and unrelated brand terms that aren't producing conversions — for example, "willandale golf cart sales" ($103.65, 0 conversions) and "ohio custom carts" ($151+, 0 conversions). Adding these as negative keywords would redirect that spend to terms that are converting.
